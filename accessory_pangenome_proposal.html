1. Your query, restated

As I understand it:

  • You have Pool-seq datasets — each pool ≈ 20 individuals — sampled across a European cline, primarily Sweden, plus Switzerland (CHF) and Spain (ES).
  • Previously these data were mapped to a single UK reference genome to call SNPs and study allele-frequency variation among populations.
  • You now want to shift to a pangenomic perspective: how much does the accessory genomic content — sequence not present in the UK reference — vary among populations?
  • Proposed strategy: for each pool, map reads to the reference, take the reads that do not map (the candidate accessory content of that population), assemble them de novo into contigs, and annotate those contigs against NCBI NR to ask whether they contain genes.
  • You expect to obtain the non-mapping reads by filtering the raw BAM file, and you are open to alternative or improved suggestions for any step.

The deliverable you asked for is this document: a restatement that demonstrates understanding, a proposed workflow with a graphical overview, and the bioinformatic decisions laid out so you can comment before we finalise a plan.

2. Goals & scientific framing

The core question is a presence/absence-and-frequency question rather than a SNP (substitution) question: what sequence does a population carry that the reference lacks, and does the amount and identity of that sequence change along the cline? I read three nested goals:

Primary

Recover and catalogue the non-reference (accessory) sequence carried by each population, and quantify how much it varies among populations and along the geographic/environmental cline.

Secondary

Ask whether accessory content is functional — does it contain protein-coding genes, gene families, or transposable elements — and whether particular elements show clinal frequency patterns that parallel the SNP-based signal.

The key conceptual reframing

Pool-seq does not give you individual genotypes — it gives population allele frequencies from pooled read counts. For accessory content this is actually a feature: the depth of coverage of a non-reference element, normalised by sequencing effort, is a proxy for the frequency × copy-number of that element in the population. So the natural unit of analysis is not "is this gene present in individual X" but "at what frequency does this accessory element segregate in population Y" — which maps perfectly onto your existing cline framework.

A consequence worth stating early: the most informative design is not "assemble each population in isolation and eyeball the differences." It is to build one common, non-redundant catalogue of accessory sequence (a mini "pan-accessory genome") and then quantify every population against that shared coordinate system. That gives you a single presence/frequency matrix (accessory element × population) that you can analyse exactly like a SNP matrix — PCA, clustering, FST-analogues, clinal regression, GO enrichment.

4. Step-by-step workflow

1

Read QC and trimming

Adapter and low-quality 3′ trimming with fastp; aggregate reports with FastQC/MultiQC. This matters more than usual here: untrimmed adapters and low-complexity tails are a classic source of spurious "unmapped" reads and junk contigs.

2

Map to the UK reference

Map trimmed reads with bwa-mem2 (you may already have these BAMs). Keep the full BAM — we need the pairing information, not just the unmapped flag.

3

Extract the non-reference reads — carefully

This is the single most consequential step, and the naive filter is a trap. samtools view -f 4 keeps only reads flagged as unmapped, which throws away the most informative class of reads. We want three categories:

  • Both mates unmapped (-f 12) — purely novel sequence.
  • One mate mapped, one unmapped — these anchor the accessory content to a reference location and are gold for understanding insertions; keep both mates of the pair.
  • Optionally: confidently-but-partially mapped reads (large soft-clips) — these mark insertion breakpoints. Use with care (see caveats).

Then re-pair and convert to FASTQ with samtools collate + samtools fastq, keeping mate relationships intact. We will write this as a small documented script rather than a one-liner, because the exact flag logic is where reproducibility lives.

Why not "just -f 4"
A read from a divergent accessory haplotype, or from the edge of an insertion, will often map partially (local alignment soft-clips the novel part) and therefore never get the unmapped flag. Mapper choice and mode (local vs end-to-end) directly change what counts as "accessory." We should fix and document this explicitly.
4

(Optional) read-level taxonomic screen

Run Kraken2 on the non-reference reads to partition sequence into (a) candidate host accessory content, (b) endosymbionts — Wolbachia, Spiroplasma, etc., (c) gut/general microbiome, and (d) human/handling contamination. We flag rather than silently delete, so every read's fate is auditable.

In-scope side result
The endosymbiont bin is not just contamination to bin away — per-population Wolbachia (and other symbiont) titre/frequency along the cline is a genuine secondary result. We will keep a clean symbiont track: estimate symbiont read fraction per pool (and, if depth allows, strain identity via MLST/Wolbachia reference mapping) and report it alongside the host accessory analysis.
5

De novo assembly — co-assembly recommended

Assemble the non-reference reads into contigs. Because the input is a mixture of multiple haplotypes (pooled individuals) plus some contamination, a metagenome-aware assembler (metaSPAdes or MEGAHIT) is more appropriate than an isolate assembler. I recommend a co-assembly of all pools to build one shared catalogue, complemented by per-population assemblies as a sensitivity check. (Trade-offs discussed in §5.)

6

Validate and decontaminate contigs

Two filters define the final catalogue:

  • Truly non-reference: align contigs back to the UK reference with minimap2/ nucmer and discard contigs that align well — these are just divergent or mis-assembled reference regions, not accessory content.
  • Not contamination: a BlobTools-style assessment (GC × coverage × taxonomy) plus Kraken2 to separate host-derived contigs from microbial/symbiont/vector sequence.

The output is the non-reference "pan-accessory" contig set — your core product.

7

Annotation

  • Homology vs NR: DIAMOND blastx against NR (far faster than NCBI BLAST; same idea).
  • Gene prediction: protein-coding ORFs and functional annotation via eggNOG-mapper and/or InterProScan (Pfam domains, GO terms).
  • Repeats/TEs: RepeatModeler+RepeatMasker — in Lepidoptera a large fraction of accessory content is expected to be transposable elements, and you'll want to separate "TE expansion" from "novel gene content."
8

Quantify across the cline

Map each population's non-reference reads back to the catalogue (bwa-mem2/minimap2), then compute, per contig per population, breadth of coverage (what fraction is covered → present/absent call) and normalised depth (proxy for population frequency). Assemble these into an element × population matrix.

9

Comparative & clinal analysis

Ordination (PCA / NMDS) and clustering of populations on the matrix; correlation of element frequency with latitude/environment (clinal regression); FST-analogues for presence/absence; and GO/Pfam enrichment of clinally-varying, gene-bearing elements. This is where the accessory-genome story connects back to your published SNP cline.

6. Key caveats & pitfalls

  • Reference bias is built in. All "accessory" content is defined relative to the UK reference. A geographically distant reference can make a population look more "accessory-rich" simply because it is more diverged, not because it carries more novel genes. We should keep this framing explicit in interpretation.
  • Pooled polymorphism fragments assemblies. Multiple haplotypes per pool create bubbles and short contigs; annotation of short fragments is weak. Expect a long tail of short, unannotatable contigs and plan length/coverage thresholds up front.
  • Coverage threshold = the result. What counts as "present" depends on the breadth/depth cutoffs and on per-pool sequencing depth. These must be set transparently and tested for sensitivity.
  • Contamination masquerades as accessory novelty. Microbiome/endosymbiont/host-handling sequence will assemble and may even carry genes. Decontamination is not optional.
  • TEs vs genes. Much accessory content will be repetitive/TE-derived. This is biologically real and interesting, but conflating it with "novel coding genes" would misstate the result — we separate them.
  • Depth ≠ frequency for repeats. High depth on a contig can mean high population frequency or high copy number. Breadth-of-coverage and per-element care mitigate this.
  • Sampling-effort confound across the cline. Sweden has more pools than CHF/ES; uneven sampling and depth must be normalised before any "Sweden has more accessory content" claim.

7. Alternative / complementary approaches

The map → assemble → annotate route you proposed is sound and interpretable. A few complementary methods are worth flagging now so we can decide scope:

Reference-free k-mer approach

Count k-mers per pool and identify k-mers absent from the reference; compare their frequencies across populations directly (k-mer GWAS-style, e.g. unitig methods). Pro: assembly-free, sidesteps fragmentation. Con: harder to attach biological meaning without assembly. Good as a cross-validation of the assembly result.

Graph pangenome

If high-quality individual assemblies (or a Swedish reference) become available, a pangenome graph (minigraph/pggb/vg) lets you genotype structural/accessory variation directly and reduces reference bias. Con: needs assemblies you may not have yet — a strong future direction rather than step one.

Anchored-insertion calling

Use the one-mate-mapped reads explicitly to localise accessory insertions onto reference coordinates (split-read / discordant-pair signals). Complements the de novo catalogue by telling you where in the genome insertions sit.

Two-reference subtraction

If a closer (e.g. Swedish) P. napi assembly exists, mapping to both and comparing "accessory" sets disentangles true novelty from reference-distance artefacts. Worth checking what assemblies are currently public.

8. Deliverables

  • A documented, reproducible pipeline (Snakemake/Nextflow) from BAM → accessory catalogue → matrix.
  • The non-reference pan-accessory contig catalogue (FASTA) with provenance and QC flags.
  • An annotation table (per contig: NR hits, Pfam/GO, TE class, length, coverage).
  • The element × population presence/frequency matrix.
  • Comparative figures: PCA/clustering, accessory-content-per-population, clinal-frequency plots, GO enrichment.
  • A symbiont track: per-population Wolbachia/endosymbiont read fraction (and strain ID where depth allows).
  • A methods write-up documenting every threshold and choice for the eventual manuscript.
Local-compute note
On a local (non-HPC) environment the two memory/runtime bottlenecks are (1) co-assembly — MEGAHIT is the safer default here as it is markedly more memory-frugal than metaSPAdes (we can still run metaSPAdes on a per-pool subset as a quality check), and (2) DIAMOND vs NR — the NR database is large (~hundreds of GB) to download and index. We should confirm available RAM and disk early; if NR is impractical locally we can target a smaller, more relevant database (e.g. Lepidoptera/arthropod RefSeq proteins or UniRef) first.
